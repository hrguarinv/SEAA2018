A Systematic Mapping on Agile Security Requirements Engineering
Secondary studie

Abstract

The rapidly changing business environment in which most companies operate is challenging traditional Requirements Engineering (RE) approaches. The term agile RE has been used to define the ‘‘agile way’’ of conducting RE activities. Security is an essential non-functional requirement that tends to be difficult to address in agile development contexts. The overall understanding of how to handle security requirements in agile contexts is still vague. [Objective] The goal of this paper is to characterize the approaches that have been proposed to handle security requirements in agile software projects. [Method] We conducted a systematic mapping study. In total, we identified 21 studies that met our inclusion criteria, dated from 2005 to 2017. [Results] We found the approaches propose solutions that may be divided into four categories: modify agile method, extend the concept of user story to abuser story, introduce a lightweight methodology and develop a software framework. We also identified some limitations of using these approaches related to environment, people, effort and resources. [Conclusion] Our analysis suggests that more effort needs to be put into empirically evaluating the solutions and develop specialized software tools in order to mitigate the identified limitations.

Coupling Metrics

Coupling measures how much a SPL component provides functionalities to other components, or depends on functionalities provided by other components. That is, coupling metrics aim at capturing when SPL components are strongly attached to the whole SPL. Thus, a high coupling suggests that the SPL components are strongly attached to other components and, therefore play an essential role in the operation of the product line. On the other hand, a low coupling suggests that the SPL component is not essential to operate the product line. In order to characterize critical components in component-based SPL, we used the following conventional coupling metrics. The first column presents the name of each metric. The second column describes the metric.

Metric Description
Coupling Between Objects (CBO) [1] Number of components coupled to one specific component
Fan-in (FANIN) [2] Number of dependencies from other components to a specific component
Fan-out (FANOUT) [2] Number of dependencies from one components to others
Depth of Inheritance Tree (DIT) [1] Maximum depth of a component in the inheritance tree
Number Of Children (NOC) [1] Number of children components of a component

Detection Strategy

The figure below presents our metric-based strategy for characterizing critical components. A detection strategy combines multiples metrics to characterize components that satisfy a property, the component criticality in our case. We assume that both FANIN and CBO explicitly capture coupling between critical components. Based on the literature of component-based SPL, a critical component is the one that has too many dependencies with other components, which we can measure with CBO > Threshold and FANIN > Threshold. However, from our viewpoint, FANOUT > Threshold may not suffice to characterize critical components because, considering the SPL architecture, each component tend to use features provided by several other components. However, when a component is deeply located in the inheritance tree and has too many children components, such external dependencies indicated by FANOUT > Threshold may negatively impact on the SPL product configuration, which makes the critical components. Thus, FANOUT has to be combined with DIT > Threshold and NOC > Threshold for capturing critical components.
